BTC Signal v50 · Execution Integrity

A manual decision aid for Robinhood BTC 15-minute contracts. Those settle on CF Benchmarks BRTI (constituents: Bitstamp, Coinbase, Gemini, itBit, Kraken, Bullish — all contributing order-book mids), so this builds a consensus from the four it can reach in a browser (Coinbase, Kraken, Bitstamp, Gemini), smooths it the way the real index is smoothed, and treats it as a proxy. It estimates your chance of finishing on the right side, tells you the live entry window, flags late reversals, catches tick-level price jumps, gates entries, and adds LateGuard endgame protection for the final minutes. Estimates are model-based, can be wrong, and are not financial advice. Use this only as a manual risk screen.

How the numbers work
Probability. Chance of finishing above target = normal CDF of ln(price / target) ÷ σ, where σ is the standard deviation of the remaining move. σ is the realized volatility of recent prices, measured on the raw consensus sampled once per second (1 Hz suppresses bid-ask microstructure noise, which otherwise inflates σ; the displayed price is separately smoothed). It's a variance rate per second × √horizon. Because the contract settles on a 60-second average, the variance horizon is shrunk to T − ⅔·window. Drift is ignored — negligible over minutes.

Reversal watch. Cushion is your distance from the target in standard deviations of the remaining move — under ~1σ is fragile. Chance it hits target is the real first-passage probability that price touches the target at least once before expiry, either way — counting any path that reaches it, even if it bounces back. It's computed from the live gap, the baseline volatility, and the recent drift: a move toward the target raises it, a move away lowers it. Using baseline σ (not a spike-inflated σ) means a volatility burst heading away from the target no longer reads as falsely high. With no drift it reduces to the reflection principle 2·Φ(−|z|), and as the clock runs out with a real gap left it falls toward 0. Volatility spike still compares the last ~20 seconds against baseline as a separate exposure flag. None of this predicts which way the next swing breaks.

Entry timing. Holding the current price and volatility, the model projects your side's confidence across every remaining minute — confidence climbs as time runs out because less price movement is left. The window opens at the first moment confidence clears your entry % with enough cushion, and closes at the no-entry blackout. Entering earlier in the window keeps more payout but a thinner cushion; later is safer but the contract is more priced-in. The whole window recomputes live, so a volatility spike pushes it later automatically. The projection is conditional — price will move, and the window moves with it.

Jump detection. Every individual tick from every feed is checked the instant it arrives: if one feed's price moves more than the "jump bps" threshold versus its own previous tick, it fires immediately — before the median or the volatility window would catch it. A single-feed jump is the earliest possible signal but can be a glitch; "confirmed" means two or more feeds moved the same way within ~1.5s. The 2-feed-confirm toggle trades a little speed for far fewer false alarms.

Speed limits — read this. This runs in a browser on public feeds. The floor on latency is the exchange pushing a message across the internet to your device (tens to low-hundreds of ms); no browser app beats that. "Measured feed lag" shows it where the exchange stamps its messages (depends on your device clock). Robinhood settles on BRTI, which only publishes once per second, so finer resolution doesn't change the outcome. This catches a jump that is already printing — nothing can warn you before one starts.

Limits. Volatility is noisy, the consensus is a BRTI proxy (not the real index), and a random walk can't anticipate news.
